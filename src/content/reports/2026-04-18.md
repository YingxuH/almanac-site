# Almanac Daily Forecast — 2026-04-18
## The Future of Software Engineering

### Executive Summary
Yesterday, we tracked the violent collision between advancing agentic infrastructure and catastrophic security realities. Today, the security baseline fundamentally cracked as NIST announced it is giving up on enriching most CVEs, leaving a massive public blind spot for vulnerability management just as AI code volume explodes. Concurrently, we are detecting the first real signals of developer fatigue and economic friction—highlighted by granular scrutiny over Claude 4.7 tokenizer costs and engineers opting to "code the old way"—even as the broader web begins building infrastructure to accommodate autonomous agents.

### Today's Top Signals

**1. NIST gives up enriching most CVEs**
The bedrock of open-source vulnerability tracking is crumbling. With the National Vulnerability Database effectively waving a white flag on enriching most entries, the industry is losing its centralized security taxonomy. For the 3-30 year outlook, this forces a massive shift toward proprietary, AI-driven SAST/DAST tools; AI code generators that rely on outdated or unenriched public vulnerability databases will inadvertently train on and propagate unpatched flaws at scale. 

**2. Measuring Claude 4.7's tokenizer costs**
Developers are hitting the financial realities of running frontier models in continuous loops. As AI delegation shifts from single-prompt autocomplete to complex, multi-step agentic workflows, tokenizer costs are compounding rapidly. This cost friction will fundamentally shape enterprise adoption over the next five years, dictating whether autonomous generation is economically viable for standard CRUD work or reserved only for high-value architectural scaffolding.

**3. Tooling emerges to scan websites for "AI agent readiness"**
The launch of tools like IsItAgentReady.com signals a permanent shift in web architecture. We are moving beyond SEO (Search Engine Optimization) to AEO (Agentic Engine Optimization). Over the next decade, developers will spend less time building visual DOMs and more time designing semantic, machine-readable interfaces specifically for autonomous software engineering agents to interact with, test, and scrape.

**4. The "coding by hand" counter-movement**
A documented sentiment backlash is emerging as developers log experiments spending months "coding the old way." This signals a looming bifurcation in the software workforce: a majority class of "AI managers" who review vast amounts of generated boilerplate, and a premium "artisanal" class focusing on deep, unassisted systems-level logic to escape AI-induced cognitive fatigue.

**5. Fil-C introduces simplified memory-safe C/C++ model**
Memory safety remains a critical bottleneck that AI cannot reliably solve without hallucination. As AI models continue to struggle with writing complex, secure C/C++ in legacy codebases, structural middleware tooling that enforces memory safety natively (like Fil-C) will become mandatory. This ensures that systems programming will remain highly specialized and resistant to total AI automation.

**6. Smol machines: subsecond coldstart, portable virtual machines**
The rise of instantaneous, subsecond virtual machines points to the future of AI containment. To mitigate the black-box security risks we observed yesterday, the next generation of IDEs and agentic workflows will execute every AI-generated function or CLI command in its own highly ephemeral, isolated VM sandbox.

**7. Terminal security degradation ("cat readme.txt" is unsafe)**
New vulnerabilities demonstrating that basic terminal commands are inherently hostile represent a catastrophic escalation vector. As AI agents increasingly rely on autonomous terminal access to build, test, and deploy code, fundamental flaws in simple bash execution environments present massive risks for automated CI/CD pipelines.
